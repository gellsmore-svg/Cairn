# Psychological Work Interface Suite

These examples show psychological processes at their interface with work,
systems, augmentation, and social context. They are descriptive process examples,
not diagnostic tools.

## PROCESS — Acute Stress Response In A High-Stakes Workflow

```cairn
PROCESS AcuteStressWorkflowResponse (INPUT: high_stakes_trigger; OUTPUT: regulated_or_dysregulated_action)
  1. Detect threat cue in work context. [HUMAN, EMOTIONAL]
     REGULATION [STRATEGY: pause_and_name_state]
     HUMAN_FACTORS:
       limbic_response: fight, flight, freeze, or fawn may become more likely under perceived threat.
       cognitive_load: working memory and flexible reasoning can narrow under acute stress.

  2. Appraise danger, control, social risk, and available support. [APPRAISAL]
     HUMAN_DEMAND:
       ORIENT: identify whether the threat is real, social, technical, or imagined.
       ACT: pause, ask, escalate, defend, avoid, comply, or repair.
       RECOVER: return to regulated task execution.

  3. Apply regulation and support before irreversible action. [REGULATION, GATED]
     SUPPORT: offer pause, second reviewer, scripted escalation, and low-shame help path.
     HUMAN_RISK:
       probability: medium
       impact: high
       confidence: medium
       rationale: high-stakes irreversible actions during threat response can amplify errors.

  4. Close loop and learn without blame. [FEEDBACK, HUMAN]
     CHANGE_IMPACT:
       reinforcement: teams learn that regulation is part of quality, not weakness.
```

## PROCESS — Borderline-Pattern Conflict Interface

```cairn
PROCESS BorderlinePatternConflictInterface (INPUT: perceived_rejection_or_instability; OUTPUT: contained_or_escalated_conflict)
  1. Perceive ambiguous feedback, delay, or social exclusion. [HUMAN, EMOTIONAL]
     HUMAN_FACTORS:
       note: this is a non-diagnostic pattern lens for process design.
       threat_sensitivity: ambiguity may be experienced as abandonment or rejection.
       social_role: team silence can intensify uncertainty.

  2. Seek reassurance, protest, withdraw, or escalate. [HUMAN, DYNAMIC]
     HUMAN_RISK:
       probability: medium
       impact: medium
       confidence: low
       rationale: interpersonal escalation can derail decisions and create secondary conflict.

  3. Use validating, boundaried response path. [HUMAN, SUPPORT]
     SUPPORT: acknowledge emotion, state facts, define next contact, and avoid vague promises.
     GAME_THEORY:
       pattern: intermittent reassurance can accidentally reinforce escalation.
       mitigation: predictable communication and consistent boundaries.

  4. Review process trigger rather than label the person. [FEEDBACK]
     HUMAN_FACTORS:
       trauma_informed: focus on cues, supports, and boundaries instead of blame.
```

## PROCESS — Narcissistic-Pattern Status Threat In Change

```cairn
PROCESS NarcissisticPatternStatusThreat (INPUT: status_threatening_change; OUTPUT: constructive_or_defensive_response)
  1. Detect status, expertise, or control threat. [APPRAISAL, HUMAN]
     HUMAN_FACTORS:
       note: this is a non-diagnostic pattern lens for work design.
       social_status: loss of special role may trigger defensiveness or devaluation of others.

  2. Respond through advocacy, obstruction, image management, or coalition seeking. [HUMAN, SOCIAL]
     GAME_THEORY:
       pattern: public challenge can reward dominance displays.
       mitigation: move from status contest to explicit criteria and private repair.

  3. Reframe change around contribution and accountable criteria. [REGULATION, COALITION]
     SUPPORT: give high-status contributors a bounded, useful role without giving veto power.
     HUMAN_RISK:
       probability: medium
       impact: high
       confidence: low
       rationale: status-threat dynamics can capture change governance and silence dissent.

  4. Monitor whether decision quality or psychological safety improves. [FEEDBACK]
```

## PROCESS — Loss Aversion At A Decision Gate

```cairn
PROCESS LossAversionDecisionGate (INPUT: proposed_stop_or_pivot; OUTPUT: continue_pivot_or_stop)
  1. Present evidence that a project should stop or pivot. [HUMAN, GATED]
     HUMAN_FACTORS:
       behavioural_economics: losses loom larger than equivalent gains.
       sunk_cost: prior investment can be treated as a reason to continue.

  2. Separate past cost from future expected value. [COGNITIVE, SUPPORT]
     SUPPORT: show future scenarios with base rates, reversibility, and opportunity cost.

  3. Decide continue, pivot, stop, or run final experiment. [HUMAN, DECISION]
     HUMAN_RISK:
       probability: high
       impact: medium
       confidence: high
       rationale: loss aversion is a predictable gate failure in product, change, and finance work.

  4. Record learning and protect reputation after stopping. [FEEDBACK]
     SUPPORT: make stopping a learning outcome when evidence justifies it.
```

## PROCESS — Caregiver Mental Load Spillover Into Work

```cairn
PROCESS CaregiverMentalLoadWorkSpillover (INPUT: caregiver_load_and_work_demands; OUTPUT: supported_or_depleted_work_capacity)
  1. Carry invisible planning, emotional, and interruption load into the workday. [HUMAN, CONTEXT]
     HUMAN_FACTORS:
       domestic_work_interface: cognitive household labour and caregiving load reduce spare executive capacity.
       intersectionality: burden may be unevenly distributed by gender, role, class, and family structure.

  2. Encounter high-focus work or system friction. [HUMAN, UI]
     HUMAN_DEMAND:
       ORIENT: understand task while managing background obligations.
       ACT: complete work, defer, ask for flexibility, or silently absorb the load.
       RECOVER: regain focus after interruption.

  3. Apply flexible support and workload design. [ORG, SUPPORT]
     SUPPORT: predictable scheduling, asynchronous handoff, clear priorities, backup coverage, and low-friction leave.
     HUMAN_RISK:
       probability: medium
       impact: high
       confidence: medium
       rationale: hidden domestic load can become quality, wellbeing, and retention risk.

  4. Review work design without surveilling private life. [FEEDBACK, ETHICAL]
     CONSTRAINTS: aggregate support needs; do not turn caregiving status into individual performance scoring.
```

## PROCESS — Antisocial-Pattern Exploitation Risk At Work

```cairn
PROCESS AntisocialPatternExploitationRisk (INPUT: low_accountability_high_reward_context; OUTPUT: contained_or_exploited_system)
  1. Detect opportunity for rule-bending, intimidation, deception, or credit capture. [APPRAISAL, HUMAN]
     HUMAN_FACTORS:
       note: this is a non-diagnostic pattern lens for process design.
       incentive_risk: weak controls and high rewards can invite exploitative behaviour.

  2. Person or group tests whether boundaries are real. [HUMAN, DYNAMIC]
     GAME_THEORY:
       pattern: if violations produce reward and little cost, exploitation becomes rational.
       mitigation: immediate, fair, documented boundary enforcement.

  3. Apply transparent controls and protect affected people. [HUMAN, GATED]
     SUPPORT: separate fact finding, psychological safety, due process, and retaliation protection.
     HUMAN_RISK:
       probability: low
       impact: high
       confidence: low
       rationale: rare exploitative patterns can cause disproportionate harm when systems reward them.

  4. Review structural incentives, not only individual behaviour. [FEEDBACK]
```

## PROCESS — Histrionic-Pattern Attention Dynamics

```cairn
PROCESS HistrionicPatternAttentionDynamics (INPUT: ambiguous_attention_or_recognition_context; OUTPUT: contained_or_disruptive_attention_cycle)
  1. Interpret attention, praise, or neglect signals. [APPRAISAL, HUMAN]
     HUMAN_FACTORS:
       note: this is a non-diagnostic pattern lens for process design.
       attention_need: ambiguous recognition can trigger dramatic bids for visibility.

  2. Group responds with attention, irritation, avoidance, or escalation. [SOCIAL, DYNAMIC]
     GAME_THEORY:
       pattern: urgent attention can be reinforced if it reliably redirects group focus.
       mitigation: predictable recognition, clear meeting structure, and private repair.

  3. Redirect to evidence, role, and contribution. [REGULATION, SUPPORT]
     SUPPORT: validate contribution without rewarding disruption or public escalation.
     HUMAN_RISK:
       probability: medium
       impact: medium
       confidence: low
       rationale: attention cycles can consume meeting capacity and distort prioritisation.

  4. Adjust norms for recognition and voice. [FEEDBACK]
```

## PROCESS — Present Bias In Planning

```cairn
PROCESS PresentBiasPlanning (INPUT: long_term_goal_with_short_term_cost; OUTPUT: protected_or_deferred_action)
  1. Compare immediate effort with delayed benefit. [HUMAN, DECISION]
     HUMAN_FACTORS:
       behavioural_economics: present bias favours immediate ease over future value.
       cognitive_load: planning is weaker when capacity is depleted.

  2. Convert distant goal into near-term trigger and commitment. [SUPPORT]
     SUPPORT: use defaults, reminders, visible progress, and smaller next actions.

  3. Act, defer, or renegotiate goal. [HUMAN, GATED]
     HUMAN_RISK:
       probability: high
       impact: medium
       confidence: high
       rationale: present bias repeatedly delays training, compliance, maintenance, and relationship repair.

  4. Feed back outcome and adjust friction. [FEEDBACK]
```
